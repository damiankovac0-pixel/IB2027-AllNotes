# A2.2.1 Network Topologies

Network topology is the arrangement of nodes and links in a network.

Topologies affect reliability, cost, scalability, speed, and fault tolerance.

## Physical vs Logical Topology

Physical topology is the real layout of devices and cables.

Logical topology is how data flows between devices.

These can differ. A network may physically look like a star but logically handle traffic in another way.

## Star Topology

In a star topology, every device connects to a central device, usually a switch or wireless access point.

Advantages:

- easy to set up and manage
- failure of one cable/device usually affects only that device
- common in homes, schools, and offices
- modern switches reduce collisions
- easy to add or remove devices

Disadvantages:

- central switch is a single point of failure
- uses more cabling than some alternatives
- central device can become a bottleneck

Practical applications:

- home networks
- small office LANs
- school computer labs
- business networks using central switches

## Mesh Topology

In a mesh topology, nodes connect to many other nodes.

Full mesh means every node connects to every other node.

Partial mesh means nodes connect to some, but not all, other nodes.

Advantages:

- high reliability
- no single central point of failure
- data can be rerouted if a link fails
- strong fault tolerance

Disadvantages:

- expensive due to many connections
- complex to configure and maintain
- full mesh becomes impractical as node count grows

Practical applications:

- backbone networks
- telecommunications networks
- military or emergency networks
- sensor networks where resilience matters

## Hybrid Topology

A hybrid topology combines two or more topologies.

Example: a large campus may use star topology inside buildings and mesh topology between buildings.

Advantages:

- flexible
- scalable
- can combine strengths of different topologies
- suitable for large and complex networks

Disadvantages:

- more complex to design
- can be expensive
- bottlenecks may occur if poorly planned

Practical applications:

- large enterprises
- university campuses
- telecommunications networks
- smart city infrastructure

## Comparison

| Topology | Strength | Weakness | Common use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Star | simple and manageable | central device failure affects all | homes, offices |
| Mesh | reliable and fault tolerant | costly and complex | backbone/resilient networks |
| Hybrid | flexible and scalable | design complexity | campuses, enterprises |

## Exam Pattern

When evaluating a topology, discuss reliability, cost, scalability, collisions, transmission speed, and failure points.
