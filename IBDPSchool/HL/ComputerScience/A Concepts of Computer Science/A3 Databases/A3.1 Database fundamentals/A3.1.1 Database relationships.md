# A3.1.1 Database Relationships

A relationship is a link between entities in a relational database.

Relationships are usually created using primary keys and foreign keys.

## Why Relationships Are Needed

Without relationships, tables exist separately and cannot easily be combined into useful information.

Example: a PATIENT table is more useful when it can be linked to the OWNER table and the TREATMENT table.

## Cardinality

Cardinality describes the number of records in one table that can be associated with records in another table.

The main types are:

- one-to-one
- one-to-many
- many-to-many

## One-To-One Relationship

A one-to-one relationship means one record in one table is related to one record in another table.

Example: one person has one passport record, and one passport record belongs to one person.

One-to-one relationships are less common because the data can often be stored in one table, unless separation is needed for security, optional data, or design clarity.

## One-To-Many Relationship

A one-to-many relationship means one record in one table can be related to many records in another table.

Example: one owner can own many pets, but each pet has one owner.

This is the most common relationship in relational databases.

Implementation:

- the primary key from the "one" side is placed as a foreign key on the "many" side

Example: `OwnerID` from OWNER is stored in PATIENT.

## Many-To-Many Relationship

A many-to-many relationship means many records in one table can relate to many records in another table.

Example: many students can enrol in many courses.

Relational databases cannot implement many-to-many relationships directly using one foreign key field.

Instead, a linking table is introduced.

## Linking Table

A linking table, also called a junction table, resolves a many-to-many relationship into two one-to-many relationships.

Example:

- STUDENT
- COURSE
- ENROLMENT

ENROLMENT contains foreign keys such as:

- StudentID
- CourseID

Each ENROLMENT record links one student to one course.

## Modality

Modality describes whether a relationship is required or optional.

## Mandatory Relationship

A mandatory relationship means the relationship must exist.

Example: a receipt must have at least one item. Without an item, it is not a valid receipt.

## Optional Relationship

An optional relationship means the relationship may exist, but does not have to.

Example: an actor may appear in a TV show, but some actors may only work in theatre.

## Relationship Summary

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| One-to-one | one record links to one record | person and passport |
| One-to-many | one record links to many records | owner and pets |
| Many-to-many | many records link to many records | students and courses |
| Mandatory | relationship must exist | receipt and items |
| Optional | relationship may exist | actor and TV show |

## Exam Pattern

For relationship questions, state the cardinality and justify it using both sides of the relationship. For many-to-many relationships, mention the need for a linking table.
