# A4.4.1 Ethical Implications Of Machine Learning

An ethical implication is a possible positive or negative consequence of a decision or action.

For machine learning, the ethical issue is often not just the prediction itself, but how the data was collected, how the model was trained, who is affected, and whether decisions can be challenged.

## Bias And Fairness

Bias occurs when a system produces unfair or distorted outcomes for certain groups.

Machine learning can inherit bias from:

- unrepresentative training data
- historical discrimination in the data
- poor [[A4.2 Data preprocessing|data preprocessing]]
- badly chosen [[A4.2.2 Feature selection|features]]
- evaluation metrics that ignore affected groups

Example: a recruitment model trained on historical hiring data may favour groups that were historically hired more often, even if better candidates exist outside those groups.

Why this matters:

- unfair rejection of applicants
- unequal access to services
- amplification of existing social inequality
- reduced trust in automated systems

Ways to reduce bias:

- use representative training data
- audit model outputs across demographic groups
- remove or carefully examine features that act as proxies for protected characteristics
- use human review for high-impact decisions
- monitor the system after deployment

## Privacy And Consent

Machine learning often needs large data sets. These may contain personal, sensitive, or identifiable information.

Ethical questions:

- Did people consent to their data being used?
- Do users understand how their data is processed?
- Is more data being collected than necessary?
- Can individuals control, delete, or correct their data?
- Is the data protected against misuse or breaches?

Privacy matters especially in healthcare, education, finance, location tracking, social media, and surveillance.

## Transparency

Transparency means people can understand how and why a system is being used.

This includes:

- what data is collected
- what the model is used for
- who has access to outputs
- whether a decision was automated
- what factors influenced the decision

Transparency is harder with complex models such as deep neural networks. In sensitive areas, a simpler but more interpretable model may be better than a slightly more accurate black-box model.

## Accountability

Accountability means there is responsibility when a system causes harm or makes a wrong decision.

Key question: who is responsible?

- the developer?
- the company?
- the user?
- the institution deploying the model?
- the data provider?

High-impact systems should have clear accountability, appeal processes, testing, documentation, and human oversight.

## Security

Machine learning systems and their data can be attacked.

Risks include:

- data breaches
- poisoned training data
- adversarial inputs designed to fool the model
- model outputs exposing private data
- unauthorized access to predictions

Security is ethical because weak security can directly harm people whose data or decisions are involved.

## Environmental Impact

Large machine learning models can require significant energy, especially during training.

Ethical questions:

- Is the environmental cost justified by the benefit?
- Could a smaller model solve the problem well enough?
- Can renewable energy or more efficient hardware be used?
- Is repeated retraining necessary?

This links to [[A4.1.2 Hardware for machine learning|hardware requirements]], because GPUs, TPUs, cloud systems, and HPC centres all consume resources.

## Online Communication

Machine learning shapes online communication through recommendation systems, content moderation, targeted advertising, and generative AI.

Potential benefits:

- filtering harmful content
- detecting cyberbullying
- recommending useful information
- translating or summarizing content

Potential harms:

- misinformation spreading quickly
- echo chambers
- manipulation through targeted content
- biased moderation
- deepfakes and impersonation
- reduced trust in digital evidence

## Sensitive Real-World Areas

### Healthcare

Benefits include earlier diagnosis and better triage.

Risks include biased diagnosis, privacy breaches, false negatives, and overreliance on automated results.

### Recruitment

Benefits include faster screening.

Risks include biased ranking, lack of transparency, and unfair rejection without appeal.

### Finance

Benefits include fraud detection and faster loan decisions.

Risks include discriminatory credit scoring and hidden use of proxy variables.

### Surveillance And Policing

Benefits include security and crime investigation.

Risks include mass surveillance, lack of consent, misidentification, chilling effects, and unfair targeting of groups.

## Exam Pattern

For a real-world scenario, discuss both benefits and risks. Strong answers mention specific issues such as bias, consent, privacy, transparency, accountability, security, environmental cost, and mitigation strategies.
