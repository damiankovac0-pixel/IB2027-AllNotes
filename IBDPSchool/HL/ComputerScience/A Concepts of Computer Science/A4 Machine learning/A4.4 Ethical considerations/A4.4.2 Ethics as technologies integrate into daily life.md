# A4.4.2 Ethics As Technologies Integrate Into Daily Life

Ethical guidelines cannot stay fixed forever because technology changes quickly and society's expectations change too.

As computer technologies become part of everyday life, ethical rules need regular reassessment.

## Why Ethics Must Be Reassessed

### Technology Changes

New capabilities create new risks.

Examples:

- generative AI can produce realistic fake media
- quantum computing may threaten current encryption
- augmented reality can collect detailed environmental and behavioural data
- pervasive AI can make automated decisions in more areas of life

### Social Values Change

What society considers acceptable can change over time.

Example: stronger privacy expectations have led to stricter data protection rules such as GDPR.

### Unintended Consequences Appear Later

Some harms are not obvious at first.

Example: social media platforms created benefits for communication, but later revealed problems such as misinformation, cyberbullying, addictive design, and polarization.

Regular reassessment allows correction when harms become visible.

## Quantum Computing

Quantum computing could solve some problems much faster than classical computers.

Possible benefits:

- scientific simulation
- drug discovery
- optimization
- materials research

Possible risks:

- breaking current cryptographic systems
- weakening digital security
- creating unequal power if only some governments or companies have access

Ethical response:

- develop quantum-resistant encryption
- plan security transitions before old systems are broken
- regulate sensitive uses

## Augmented Reality And Virtual Reality

AR overlays digital information onto the real world. VR creates immersive digital environments.

Possible benefits:

- education and training
- medical simulation
- remote collaboration
- accessibility support

Possible risks:

- privacy from eye tracking, movement tracking, and environment scanning
- manipulation through immersive advertising
- psychological effects
- harassment in virtual spaces
- exclusion of people without access to expensive devices

## Pervasive AI

Pervasive AI means AI systems becoming embedded throughout everyday life.

Examples:

- smart homes
- recommendation systems
- workplace monitoring
- automated customer service
- school analytics
- health tracking
- transport systems

Benefits:

- convenience
- personalization
- efficiency
- better prediction and automation

Risks:

- constant surveillance
- loss of autonomy
- hidden decision-making
- dependence on automated systems
- unequal access
- normalization of data collection

## Mitigating Risks

Risk mitigation means reducing the likelihood or impact of harm.

Important strategies:

- proactive regulation
- transparency requirements
- data minimization
- privacy-by-design
- security-by-design
- bias audits
- human oversight
- appeal processes
- environmental impact checks
- public education
- international cooperation for technologies that cross borders

## Balancing Benefits And Rights

The overall ethical challenge is balancing innovation with individual rights.

Important rights and values include:

- privacy
- autonomy
- fairness
- safety
- access
- accountability
- freedom from discrimination

## Exam Pattern

When discussing integrated technologies, explain that ethical guidelines must be reassessed because technology, social values, and unintended consequences change over time. Then connect the technology to specific risks and realistic mitigation strategies.
