# B1.1.1 Problem Specification

A problem specification defines the problem to be solved before building a solution. It ensures the developer, client, and stakeholders share the same understanding.

## Components

A complete problem specification usually includes:

1. problem statement
2. constraints and limitations
3. objectives and goals
4. input specifications
5. output specifications
6. evaluation criteria

## Problem Statement

A problem statement describes the problem, who it affects, and why it matters.

A good problem statement is:
- focused on a specific issue
- measurable
- linked to a negative consequence or opportunity

Poor example:

> "Our customer service processes are inefficient."

This is too vague. Inefficient could mean anything.

Good example:

> "Customers wait an average of 25 minutes on hold, leading to a 15% increase in abandoned calls over the past quarter."

This identifies the specific issue, provides metrics, and names the business impact.

## Constraints And Limitations

Constraints are restrictions on the solution.

They may include:
- budget
- time
- technology
- legal requirements (e.g. GDPR)
- platform compatibility
- hardware limits
- personnel

Examples:
- "The solution must run on existing hardware."
- "The system must comply with local data protection laws."
- "The project must be completed within six months."
- "The application must support languages and currencies for global deployment."

## Objectives And Goals

A high-level objective describes the broad desired outcome.
A specific goal is measurable and time-constrained.

Example:

| Objective | Specific goal |
| --- | --- |
| Enhance customer experience by minimizing wait times | Decrease average phone hold time by 20% within one quarter |
| Improve customer satisfaction | Achieve average satisfaction rating of 4/5 on post-service surveys over six months |

## Input Specifications

Input specifications describe what data the system will receive.

Consider:
- format (text, voice, barcode, sensor reading)
- source (manual entry, automatic, direct entry)
- constraints (valid ranges, required fields)
- expected characteristics (clear, concise, unambiguous)

Examples:
- text-based input via chat interface
- sensor readings with temperature range -10 to 50 degrees Celsius
- customer feedback in free-text form

## Output Specifications

Output specifications describe what the system should produce.

Consider:
- format (text, report, dashboard, response)
- content (what information must be included)
- presentation (layout, readability, constraints)

Examples:
- "The chatbot must produce direct text responses to customer enquiries."
- "Reports must display average satisfaction scores, NPS, and wait times by channel."
- "Output must be filterable by date range and topic."

## Evaluation Criteria

Evaluation criteria define how to measure success.

Typical categories:

- effectiveness (did it solve the problem?)
- efficiency (time savings, reduced manual work)
- usability (can users actually use it?)
- maintainability (easy to update?)
- reliability (does it work consistently?)
- scalability (can it grow?)

## Exam Pattern

For problem specification questions, ensure all six components are addressed. A specification missing input or output details is incomplete.
