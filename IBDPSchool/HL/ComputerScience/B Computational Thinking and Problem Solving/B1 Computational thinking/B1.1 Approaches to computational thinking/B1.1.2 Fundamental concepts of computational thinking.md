# B1.1.2 Fundamental Concepts Of Computational Thinking

Computational thinking rests on four fundamental concepts:

1. decomposition
2. pattern recognition
3. abstraction
4. algorithmic design

## Decomposition

Decomposition is breaking a complex problem into smaller, manageable parts.

Each part can be solved separately and then combined into a whole solution.

Guiding questions:
- Can I divide this problem into smaller steps?
- Are there natural phases or sequences?
- What does a diagram or mind map of the problem look like?
- Which parts are independent and can be solved in isolation?

Example: designing a video game NPC

Breaking it down:
- sensing (perceive player and surroundings)
- decision-making (evaluate situations, choose actions)
- actions (patrol, attack, flee, hide)
- pathfinding (navigate the environment)

## Pattern Recognition

Pattern recognition is identifying similarities, trends, and repeated structures within a problem.

Finding patterns allows you to:
- reuse solutions
- predict behaviour
- group related cases
- spot anomalies

Guiding questions:
- Are there similarities between parts of the problem?
- Does anything repeat?
- Have I seen a problem like this before?
- Are there common functions performed by different parts?

Example in healthcare scheduling: analysing historical data reveals peak trauma times at night, elective surgeries in the morning, and typical length-of-stay patterns per condition.

## Abstraction

Abstraction is filtering out unnecessary details to focus on the essential elements.

It means creating a simplified model of a complex system.

Guiding questions:
- What is the core purpose of the system?
- What are the essential inputs and outputs?
- If I remove a component, does the system still achieve its purpose?
- Can I replace specific details with variables?
- What can be ignored?

Example: predicting a football match result — the colour of players' shoelaces is irrelevant, but recent injuries and past performance between teams are important.

Example: a map is an abstraction of a territory. The London Tube map shows stations and connections but not actual geography, building locations, or street layouts.

## Algorithmic Design

Algorithmic design is creating a clear, step-by-step sequence of instructions to solve a problem.

An algorithm must be:
- precise
- unambiguous
- finite
- correct

Guiding questions:
- What are the inputs?
- What are the expected outputs?
- Are there decision points?
- Are there loops or repetitions?
- Can I break this into smaller steps?
- Can I explain this to someone with limited knowledge of my solution?

Example: predicting the next number in the sequence 2, 4, 6, 8:

```
1. Get current number
2. Add 2 to current number
3. Output the new number
```

## How The Concepts Work Together

These concepts are not independent steps followed in order. A good computational thinker moves between them:

1. Decompose the problem to understand its structure.
2. Identify patterns within and across the parts.
3. Abstract away irrelevant detail for each part.
4. Design algorithms for each part.
5. Combine the parts into a complete solution.
